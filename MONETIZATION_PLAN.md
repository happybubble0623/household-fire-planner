## The founder's stance (read this first)

This plan is deliberately narrow. Two honest assumptions drive everything below:

1. **The product is relatively simple.** It's a clean, free planning workspace — not a sprawling SaaS. It doesn't need (and shouldn't grow) a heavy paid-feature surface to justify itself.
2. **Willingness to pay is expected to be low.** People reach for a free FIRE calculator precisely because they don't want to pay. A small, simple tool is unlikely to convert many of them into subscribers no matter how it's packaged.

Given that, the founder is **only interested in low-maintenance monetization** — things you **set up once and largely leave alone**. Explicitly *not* on the menu: building and supporting paywalled features, running a sales process, or committing to ongoing content/ops work. Each of those is a recurring job, and the expected payoff doesn't justify the job.

**So the optimization target is: set-and-forget, cost-recovery, zero trust damage.** The realistic goal is to **cover hosting and the domain** and earn **modest side income** on top — *not* to build a profitable business. Anything that demands continuous attention gets parked, not pursued.

---

## The non-negotiable lens

Every option is still judged against the four promises the brand is built on:

1. **Free** — the core planning experience stays genuinely free, not a crippled teaser.
2. **Clean & ad-free** — no clutter, no popups, no chasing you around the web.
3. **Privacy-first, no login required** — numbers stay in the browser; nothing to link, no account forced.
4. **Plain language, no sales pressure** — this is a workspace, not a funnel into someone's commission.

If a money idea dents any of these, it has to earn its place loudly — and most don't. **Never spend trust faster than you build it.** A single sleazy placement can undo months of goodwill, and the FIRE community has a long memory.

# Recommended core — low-maintenance, set-and-forget

## 1. Donations / "buy me a coffee" / optional supporter tip

**How it works.** A simple, no-pressure "support this project" link — Buy Me a Coffee, Ko-fi, GitHub Sponsors, or a Stripe one-tap. One-time, **no accounts**, no recurring billing for you to own. Framing matters: "*This tool is free and ad-free. If it helped, you can chip in to keep it that way.*" Optionally a tiny "supporter" thank-you list — but no gated features, so it stays clean.

**Maintenance: very low — the textbook set-and-forget option.** A link and a line of copy. The platform handles payments, receipts, and support. Once it's live there is essentially nothing to manage.

**Realistic revenue.** Low and lumpy — a few dollars per *thousands* of visitors. Early/moderate traffic: **$0–low tens/mo**. At scale with a loyal audience: **low hundreds/mo** in good months. It won't pay the bills, but it directly serves the cost-recovery goal and costs nothing to keep.

**Trust / UX: very low risk — actually trust-*building*.** A polite, dismissible "keep it free and ad-free" ask *reinforces* the positioning. The only failure mode is over-asking (popups, guilt-trips) — keep it to a quiet footer / about-page placement.

**Recommendation:** **Do this now.** Lowest effort, on-brand, signals values. Treat the money as a bonus.

---

## 2. Affiliate / referral links

**How it works for this product.** Plan My FIRE surfaces the exact moments where a user realizes they need a financial product: "I should open a Roth," "I need a higher-yield place for my cash bridge," "I need ACA coverage for the pre-65 gap," "I should consolidate old 401(k)s." A *contextual, clearly-labeled* "learn more" link to a relevant provider can pay a referral fee when a user signs up. The fit is genuine because the need is real and surfaced by the user's own numbers — not invented.

**The low-maintenance way to do this — and the only way the founder wants to.** This is *not* "build an affiliate program." It's: place **a handful of relevant, clearly-disclosed contextual links once**, then leave them. No ongoing management, no rotating offers, no performance dashboards to babysit, no "our partners" page to curate. Pick a few good fits, link them at the natural moment, disclose, and move on.

Good categories, roughly **lowest-effort to set up first**:
- **Brokerages / IRA providers** — large, well-run affiliate programs (often via mainstream networks); easy to join once there's real content, stable links that rarely change. *Lowest-friction starting point.*
- **High-yield savings** — straightforward programs, clean fit for the "cash bridge" moment, simple links. *Also low-effort.*
- **Budgeting / planning tools** — usually simple self-serve affiliate signups; easy links.
- **ACA / health-insurance marketplaces** (e.g. a licensed broker) — genuinely relevant to the pre-65 gap, but vetting the partner takes a little more care, so set this up after the easier three.

**Maintenance: low**, *if you hold the line on "place once, leave alone."* The one genuinely required, recurring-in-spirit obligation is keeping the disclosure accurate — but that's a static page plus a footer line, not ongoing work. Avoid anything that turns this into a job (constantly chasing better payouts, A/B testing placements, managing dozens of partners).

**Realistic revenue.** Scales with *qualified clicks → conversions*, not pageviews. Early (~1–5k visitors/mo): essentially noise, **$0–low tens/mo**. Moderate (~20–50k/mo): **low hundreds/mo**, since funded-account payouts can be $50–$200+. It can become the largest of the low-maintenance streams, but only at real traffic — and even then it stays "modest side income," consistent with the low pay-willingness expectation.

**The non-negotiable caveats.**
- **FTC disclosure is mandatory, not optional.** This is financial content with paid relationships. Add a standing disclosure (footer + a short line near any affiliate link) and an `/affiliate-disclosure` page. Set this up *with* the links, once.
- **No biased "recommendations," no advice.** Never let payouts shape what's shown. Keep language to "here are options people in your situation use" — never "you should buy X." No personalized recommendations that could imply fiduciary advice. The instant a user suspects commission is driving placement, the whole positioning collapses — and the FIRE community punishes anything that smells like advisor sales.
- **Keep it sparse and contextual.** One tasteful, relevant link at a moment of genuine need beats a wall of partners. Sparse placement *protects* trust and conversion quality at the same time — and happens to be the least work.

**Recommendation:** **Do this now, lightly.** A few disclosed, contextual links placed once — that's the whole play. Start with the lowest-friction categories (brokerage, high-yield savings) and stop there unless traffic clearly justifies more.

## 3. A simple, static "Support this tool" page

**How it works.** One plain, static page (`/support` or similar) that houses the donation link (§1), the FTC affiliate disclosure (§2), and a short, honest note: *this tool is free and ad-free; here's how it's kept running.* It's the single home for "how you can help" without cluttering the workspace.

**Maintenance: very low.** Write it once. It doubles as the required disclosure surface, so it pulls double duty for no extra ongoing effort.

**Recommendation:** **Do this now**, alongside 1 and 2 — it's where they live. Keep it quiet (footer link), plain-language, no hard sell.

# Parked — higher maintenance, revisit only if traffic/demand proves it

These are kept for the record, not deleted — the analysis is sound and may matter later. Each is parked for one reason: **it's a recurring job**, and given a simple product and low expected willingness to pay, the ongoing effort isn't justified yet. Revisit only if traffic or demand clearly proves the case.

## Pro / freemium subscription tier

*Why parked: building and supporting paywalled features, billing, and customer support is high ongoing maintenance — and demand is uncertain for a simple tool whose users are expected to have low willingness to pay.*

**The idea (preserved).** Keep the entire current experience free forever; "Pro" is additive convenience for power users — saved/compare scenarios, PDF/shareable report export, advanced projections (Monte Carlo, sequence-of-returns, deeper Roth-ladder modeling), priority data refresh, optional cross-device sync. Free-tool conversion is typically ~1–3% of *engaged* users and lower for a tool used a few times rather than daily; at scale a strong power-user base could reach four figures/mo, but only with compelling depth.

**Why it stays parked.** It fails the low-maintenance test on every axis: auth, Stripe billing, entitlement gating, and — most importantly — an open-ended **support obligation** the moment anyone pays. Paid users expect reliability and responsiveness; that's a standing commitment, not a one-time setup. Combined with low expected willingness to pay, the effort-to-reward ratio doesn't clear the bar. **Revisit only** if a real base of repeat power users emerges *and* they're visibly asking to pay for depth.

## B2B / white-label / embeddable calculators

*Why parked: high-touch sales and ongoing client support — the opposite of set-and-forget.*

**The idea (preserved).** The calculators and household model could be licensed/embedded by fee-only advisors, employers/benefits teams, or finance publishers, or offered as a widget/API. Deals are few but worth far more than consumer micro-payments ($X00–$X,000+ per client/yr), and it lives on a **separate B2B surface**, leaving the consumer app clean and untouched.

**Why it stays parked.** Revenue is **sales-driven**, not traffic-driven — which means productizing for embedding (theming, isolation, licensing) *plus* running a sales motion *plus* supporting paying clients. That's a different business with its own continuous effort curve. **Revisit only** if inbound interest appears unprompted (e.g. an advisor asks to embed it) — never build it speculatively.

## Sponsorships / sponsored newsletter

*Why parked: it requires running and growing a newsletter — ongoing content and ops work — before there's anything to sell.*

**The idea (preserved).** With an audience (email list and/or steady traffic), sell *curated, vetted* sponsorship — a single tasteful "supported by" slot or a sponsor in a periodic FIRE newsletter. You control who appears (a reputable brokerage or HSA provider, not a payday lender) with no tracking-script baggage. Finance newsletter sponsorship runs roughly $20–40 per 1,000 engaged subscribers per send; a 10k+ engaged list can mean mid-hundreds to low-four-figures per send.

**Why it stays parked.** The money is real, but it's downstream of **building and publishing a newsletter people want** — a recurring content engine, the single biggest ongoing-work commitment in this whole document. That's squarely what the founder wants to avoid. **Revisit only** if an opt-in list happens to grow on its own and sustaining a newsletter starts to feel worth it.

## What to avoid (explicitly off the table)

These conflict fatally with the brand and are non-starters regardless of maintenance level:

- **Display ads / programmatic ad networks.** The positioning is *literally* "clean, ad-free." Ad networks drop tracking/retargeting cookies (a direct hit to the privacy promise), the finance ads served are often the exact predatory products the audience distrusts, and they clutter and slow the workspace. The modest revenue never justifies breaking an explicit brand promise. **No ads.**
- **Selling or sharing user financial data.** Off the table, permanently. The entire pitch is "your numbers stay yours, in your browser." Selling data would be a total betrayal — and legally/reputationally radioactive for finance data. **Never.**
- **Forcing login or account creation to use core features.** The no-login promise is a core differentiator. Don't gate the free workspace behind sign-up to harvest emails.
- **Commission-driven "recommendations" disguised as neutral advice.** Biased rankings dressed as guidance are the exact thing the audience distrusts, and risk looking like regulated financial advice.
- **Dark patterns:** fake urgency, deliberately crippling a free tier, nag-walls, hard-to-cancel subscriptions, pre-checked opt-ins. All erode the trust everything depends on.
- **Lead-gen to commissioned/AUM advisors or low-quality finance products** (payday loans, high-fee annuities, "get rich" pitches). Wrong audience, wrong values.

## Recommended phased path

Deliberately light. The whole point is to avoid signing up for recurring work before there's any evidence it would pay off.

### Phase 1 — Now
**Set up the low-maintenance core, then leave it running.**
- ✅ Add a quiet, no-pressure **donation / "keep it free and ad-free" link** (§1).
- ✅ Place **a few disclosed, contextual affiliate links** (§2) at genuine decision moments — start with the lowest-friction categories (brokerage, high-yield savings). Sparse, labeled, never biased.
- ✅ Ship a simple, static **"Support this tool" page** (§3) that houses the donation link and the FTC affiliate disclosure.
- ⛔ Nothing else. No paywall, no newsletter, no sales process, no ads, no forced login.

That's the plan. Everything in Phase 1 is set-and-forget; once it's live, the day-to-day is zero.

### Phase 2 — Only if traffic grows a lot
**Add depth where it's still low-effort; reconsider a parked option only if demand is now obvious.**
- ✅ Add **more affiliate depth** — a few more relevant, disclosed links / categories (e.g. ACA marketplace, budgeting tools) — still placed-once, still sparse.
- 🔎 **Revisit a single parked option** *only* if the traffic/demand evidence is clear (e.g. advisors asking to embed → B2B; a power-user base visibly asking to pay → Pro; an organically grown list → newsletter). Pick at most one, and only if you're genuinely willing to take on its ongoing work.
- ⛔ Still no ads, no forced login.

There is no assumption that a Pro tier (or any parked option) gets built soon. It happens only if reality demands it.

## Principles — monetize without betraying the promise

1. **Low-maintenance only.** If it can't be set up once and largely left alone, it doesn't ship — at least not now. Recurring work (paywalled features, sales, newsletters) is parked until demand proves it.
2. **Cost-recovery, not profit.** The goal is covering hosting/domain plus modest side income. Modest revenue from gentle, on-brand streams is the *success* case, not a disappointment.
3. **The free core stays genuinely free — forever.** All calculators, all three strategy modes, the household view, the no-login workspace. Monetization is *additive* (a tip jar, a few optional links), never *subtractive*.
4. **Privacy is not for sale.** No selling data, no forced accounts, no third-party tracking smuggled in via ads. The no-login path always works.
5. **Disclose everything.** Affiliate relationships and any paid placement get clear, conspicuous labels. Transparency is on-brand *and* legally required for finance content.
6. **Never look like biased financial advice.** Surface options, disclose relationships, keep the language "here's what people in your situation consider" — never "you should buy this."
7. **Calm over clutter, trust over revenue.** Sparse and relevant beats dense and lucrative. Never spend trust faster than you earn it.

---

## A realistic expectations note

Be honest about the numbers: **low-maintenance routes yield modest revenue.** A donation link and a handful of affiliate links on a simple, free tool — used by people who reached for it *because* they don't want to pay — will earn a little, not a lot. Early on it's effectively a rounding error; with real traffic it might grow to tens → low hundreds/mo.

**That's acceptable, by design.** The goal here is cost-recovery (hosting + domain) plus modest side income, and the founder's working assumption is that willingness to pay is low. Trying to force more revenue would mean exactly the recurring work — paywalls, sales, a newsletter — that this plan is built to avoid. The trade is intentional: **less money, near-zero ongoing effort, zero trust damage.** Build the trust and the audience first; let the small, on-brand revenue follow on a lag.
